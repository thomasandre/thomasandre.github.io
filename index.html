Global Trajectory Optimisation Competition

Successful Solution!

This summer, I participated in the 8th edition of the Global Trajectory Optimisation Competition (GTOC 8). GTOC is a competition where "the best aerospace engineers and mathematicians world wide challenge themselves to solve as 'nearly impossible' problem of interplanetary trajectory design"   This year's theme was "High resolution mapping of radio souces in the universe using space-based very long baseline interferometry (VLBI)"

The other teams were composed of seasoned industry professionals with advanced degrees and university researchers. I completed this on my own, following my first full year as an undergraduate student. Some teams had more than ten members. As far as I can tell, I am the only undergraduate to submit a successful solution since the first GTOC was conceived.

OpenRocket-ActEC (Active Energy Control)

This is a testbed for high-powered rocket flight computers that control active energy elements during the boost/coast phase of a rocket. Derivative work of the open source OpenRocket and Dispersion software projects.

ActEC is intended to torture test candidate control algorithms. It exploits the medium-fidelity flight simulation capabilities of OpenRocket to provide a realistic environment against which a candidate controller can be tested.

ActEC emulates onboard electronic sensors that can provide input to a controller. Their dynamics can be 'fuzzed' such that their outputs are distorted to emulate real-world noise and other imperfections. The flight conditions (including air temperate, density, etc.) as well as the physical properties/functionalities of the rocket and its control system can be 'fuzzed' to model inherent imperfections in real-world measurements. ActEC provides immediate, realistic controller performance feedback that can augment/replace real test flight data.
